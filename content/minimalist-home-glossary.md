# Large Minimalist Home Glossary
> 40 terms for understanding, designing, and negotiating large minimalist custom homes. Chicago and Midwest focus. By Dan Cruz, NOD — negotiatorsondemand.com

---

## A

### Architectural Precast
Concrete panels cast off-site and assembled on-site. Used in minimalist homes for clean, monolithic wall planes. $35–$55/sqft installed in Chicago.

---

## B

### Bid Set
The construction document package sent to GCs for pricing. For custom minimalist homes, bid sets must be unusually detailed — minimalist detailing has no trim to hide gaps. Incomplete bid sets cause 15–30% cost overruns.

### Black Frame / Dark Frame Glazing
Floor-to-ceiling windows with dark aluminum or steel frames. Signature of modern minimalist architecture. Chicago cost: $180–$350/sqft of glazing for thermally broken commercial-grade systems. Residential-grade systems are cheaper ($80–$120/sqft) but look chunkier.

### Board-Formed Concrete
Concrete cast against rough-sawn wood boards, leaving a wood-grain texture on the finished surface. Used for accent walls, fireplaces, and exterior walls. $25–$40/sqft premium over smooth-form.

### Budget Contingency
The reserve fund in a construction budget. For custom minimalist homes: 15–20% (vs. 10% for standard construction). Minimalist detailing has lower margin for error — a 1/8" misalignment in drywall is a punch-list item; the same misalignment in a flush base detail is a redo.

---

## C

### Cantilever
A structural element extending horizontally beyond its support. Used in minimalist design for floating stairs, projecting balconies, and extended roof planes. Requires structural engineering — rule of thumb: can cantilever 1/3 of the supported span.

### Clerestory
A band of high windows above eye level. Brings light deep into a floor plan without sacrificing wall space for art or privacy. Common in minimalist homes.

### CMU (Concrete Masonry Unit)
Concrete block. Often used as the structural core of ICF homes or as exposed interior walls in industrial-minimalist designs. Polished or painted CMU is a $12–$18/sqft interior finish vs. $3–$5/sqft for drywall.

### Cold Roof / Warm Roof
Cold roof = insulation at ceiling level, attic is ventilated. Warm roof = insulation at roof deck, attic is conditioned. Most minimalist flat-roof designs are warm roofs to keep the roof line clean. Requires careful vapor barrier detailing in Chicago's climate.

### Cost Per Square Foot — Custom Minimalist
Chicago range: $350–$650/sqft for a well-executed large minimalist home (4,000+ sqft). Entry-level custom: $250–$350. High-end architect-designed: $650–$1,000+. These are all-in numbers: hard costs + soft costs, excluding land.

---

## D

### Design-Build
A single contract with one entity for both design and construction. Can work well for minimalist homes if the design-build firm has a strong minimalist portfolio. Risk: fewer checks and balances than design-bid-build.

### Design Development (DD)
The middle phase between schematic design and construction documents. Where systems are coordinated, materials are selected, and the budget is refined. In minimalist homes, DD is where alignment (flush planes, continuous reveals, zero-trim details) gets resolved — skipping DD detail is the #1 cause of field problems.

---

## E

### ERV (Energy Recovery Ventilator)
A balanced ventilation system that exchanges indoor and outdoor air while recovering heat and moisture. Essential in tightly sealed minimalist homes, which otherwise trap humidity and CO2. $4,000–$8,000 installed.

---

## F

### Fenestration
The arrangement of windows and doors on a building's façade. Minimalist fenestration favors large expanses of glass, aligned openings, and restrained rhythm. Chicago energy code limits glazing to 30% of wall area without trade-offs.

### Flat Roof / Low-Slope Roof
A roof with minimal pitch (typically 1/4" per foot for drainage). Standard in minimalist architecture. Chicago requires membrane roofing (EPDM, TPO, PVC) or fluid-applied systems. Not a weak point if detailed correctly — but Chicago's freeze-thaw cycles are unforgiving on bad flat-roof details.

### Floating Stair
A staircase where treads appear to cantilever from the wall with no visible stringer. Steel spine + wood or concrete treads. $15,000–$40,000 for a single-flight floating stair. Code requires a guardrail if the open side exceeds 30" drop.

### Flush Base Detail
Where the wall meets the floor with no visible baseboard — the drywall stops 1/4"–1/2" above the floor, creating a shadow line. Looks clean, costs 3–4x standard baseboard. Requires floor installed before drywall finishing + precise leveling. The detail that separates amateur from pro minimalist builders.

### Flush Door / Hidden Door
A door with no visible frame or casing — the door plane is flush with the adjacent wall plane. Typically uses concealed hinges (Soss, Tectus) and a magnetic latch. $1,500–$4,000 per door installed vs. $300–$500 for a standard pre-hung door.

---

## G

### Glazing Ratio
The percentage of a wall that is glass. High glazing ratios (50%+) are a signature of minimalist design but conflict with energy codes and HVAC sizing. Chicago's climate makes high-glazing homes require careful solar gain modeling.

### Ground Face Block
CMU with the outer face ground smooth to expose the aggregate. Used as an interior finish wall in industrial-minimalist homes. $14–$22/sqft installed. Cheaper than stone, warmer than painted block.

---

## H

### Heated Slab / Radiant Slab
Concrete floor with embedded hydronic tubing for heating. Popular in minimalist homes with exposed concrete floors. Chicago installed cost: $8–$14/sqft. Most comfortable heat source — but slow to respond (hours, not minutes).

---

## I

### ICF (Insulated Concrete Forms)
Hollow foam blocks stacked, reinforced with rebar, and filled with concrete. Creates a monolithic concrete wall with continuous insulation (R-22+). Ideal for minimalist homes: excellent thermal performance, sound isolation, and a structure that naturally supports large openings. Chicago cost: $28–$42/sqft of wall area.

### Integrated / Concealed Shade Pocket
A recess in the ceiling or wall header that hides a motorized shade when retracted. Avoids the visual clutter of exposed roller tubes. $800–$1,500 per window vs. $200–$400 for surface-mounted. Specify in framing, not as an afterthought.

---

## L

### Light Shelf
A horizontal reflective surface placed below a clerestory or high window that bounces daylight deeper into the space. Passive daylighting technique, no moving parts, one framing detail. Good bang-for-buck in a minimalist home.

### Linear Drain
A long, narrow floor drain (typically in showers) instead of a round center drain. Allows a single-slope floor plane — no four-way pitch breaking the floor plane. $400–$1,200 vs. $80 for a center drain. Requires the floor to be set with the drain location before tile.

---

## M

### Mass Timber / CLT (Cross-Laminated Timber)
Engineered wood panels made from layers of dimensional lumber glued crosswise. Used for floors, roofs, and walls. Emerging in US residential, well-established in Europe. Fire-rated, carbon-sequestering, and visually warm. Chicago cost: $35–$55/sqft for CLT floor/roof panels.

### MEP Chase
A vertical cavity running through the house for mechanical, electrical, and plumbing runs. In minimalist design, chases must be planned in schematic design — there's no soffit or bulkhead to hide a duct run later.

### Monolithic Finish
A single-material surface with minimal visible joints. Examples: full-slab porcelain backsplash, troweled concrete floor, seamless epoxy coating. Expensive because it requires large-format materials and precise substrate preparation.

### Mullion / Transom
A mullion is the vertical divider between window units; a transom is the horizontal divider. Minimalist glazing favors narrow-sightline mullions (1"–2") or butt-glazed corners (no mullion at all — structurally glazed). Narrow mullions are 2–3x the cost of standard.

---

## P

### Passive House / PHIUS
A building standard for extreme energy efficiency: airtight envelope, minimal thermal bridging, mechanical ventilation with heat recovery. PHIUS certification in Chicago adds $15–$25/sqft. Alignment with minimalist design is natural — both value clean envelope detailing.

### Punch Window
A window set into a wall opening, surrounded by wall on all four sides. Contrast with window walls, where the glazing spans floor-to-ceiling. Minimalist homes use mostly window walls and clerestories; punch windows are used sparingly for composition.

---

## R

### R-Value
Thermal resistance. Chicago Code minimum: R-20 walls, R-49 roof. ICF walls deliver R-22 to R-28 continuous (no thermal bridging). Mass timber or steel-frame homes need careful detailing to avoid thermal bridging through the structure.

### Rain Screen
A cladding system with an air gap between the exterior finish and the water-resistive barrier. Allows drainage and drying. Required for any minimalist exterior that isn't monolithic concrete — including fiber cement, metal panel, and wood cladding.

### Reveal
A continuous groove or gap between two surfaces. The most common minimalist detail: a 1/4"–1/2" reveal between wall and floor, wall and ceiling, or wall and door frame. A reveal requires consistent gapping across long runs — framers and drywallers must understand this is a finish detail, not a trim-coverable tolerance.

### Shadow Line
A reveal deep enough to create a visible shadow (typically 1/2"–3/4"). Deeper than a standard reveal, used where the shadow itself is the design feature — ceiling-to-wall transitions, base details, cabinet pulls. Requires blocking and coordination in framing.

---

## S

### SIP (Structural Insulated Panel)
A sandwich of OSB and rigid foam used for walls and roofs. Faster than stick-framing, better insulation, less thermal bridging. SIP homes can achieve Passive House performance without Passive House complexity. Chicago cost: $16–$24/sqft of wall area (material only).

### Specification Book / Spec Book
The written document that accompanies the drawings, specifying every material, product, and installation standard. For a custom minimalist home, the spec book is 80–120 pages. Without it, the GC will substitute cheaper materials that break the minimalist intent — and the contract won't stop them.

### Steel Frame (Light-Gauge / Structural)
Light-gauge steel studs for non-load-bearing walls; structural steel (W-sections, HSS) for long spans and cantilevers. Steel is dimensionally stable (no shrinkage, no warp), which serves minimalist detailing well. Chicago cost premium over wood framing: 20–35%.

---

## T

### Thermal Break / Thermal Bridge
A thermal bridge is a path of low thermal resistance through the building envelope (steel stud, concrete slab edge, cantilevered balcony). A thermal break interrupts that path with insulating material. In Chicago's climate, unbroken thermal bridges cause condensation and 15–30% energy loss. Steel-frame and concrete homes require thermal breaks at every structural penetration.

### Thermally Broken Glazing
Window/door frames with a non-conductive barrier between interior and exterior metal. Essential in Chicago for high-glazing minimalist homes — without it, interior frame surfaces condense in winter. Chicago cost adder: $40–$80/sqft of glazing over non-thermal frames.

---

## V

### Vapor Barrier / Vapor Retarder
A material that limits water vapor diffusion through the building envelope. In Chicago (Climate Zone 5A), the vapor barrier goes on the warm-in-winter side of the wall (interior side). Incorrect vapor barrier placement causes condensation inside the wall — a catastrophic and common custom-home defect.

---

## W

### Window Wall
A non-load-bearing wall made predominantly of floor-to-ceiling glass, typically site-assembled from individual units. Different from curtain wall (which spans floors and is hung from the structure). Standard in minimalist homes for the rear elevation and courtyard-facing walls. Chicago cost: $120–$250/sqft for mid-range systems.

---

## Z

### Zero-Threshold Shower
A shower with no curb — the bathroom floor and shower floor are the same plane. Achieved by recessing the shower pan into the floor framing or slab. $1,500–$3,000 premium over a curbed shower. Requires a 2" drop from drain to top of curb-equivalent — plan for it in framing, not tile.
