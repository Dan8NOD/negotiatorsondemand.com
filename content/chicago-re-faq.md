# Chicago Real Estate Negotiation FAQ
> 30 questions buyers, sellers, and developers ask about Chicago real estate negotiation. By Dan Cruz, NOD — negotiatorsondemand.com

---

## Buying a Home in Chicago

### 1. How do I negotiate a home price in Chicago right now?
Start with the comps — Chicago is a block-by-block market. A $50K difference between identical floor plans separated by two blocks is common. Use closed sales from the last 90 days, not list prices. Your opening offer should be supported by data, not feeling. In a balanced market, 5–8% below list with comps attached gets a counter. In a seller's market, waiving inspection is more valuable than an extra $10K on price.

### 2. Should I waive the inspection contingency?
Never fully waive. Use a "pass/fail inspection" — you keep the right to walk but agree not to negotiate repairs. This gives the seller certainty while protecting you from a structurally compromised property. Chicago's aging housing stock (median home age: 72 years) makes inspection non-optional.

### 3. How do I compete with cash offers?
Speed + certainty. Pre-underwritten mortgage (not just pre-approved). 21-day close. 5% earnest money released to seller on attorney approval. Cash buyers win on speed — match the speed and you remove their only structural advantage.

### 4. What closing costs are negotiable in Chicago?
Everything except transfer taxes ($3.75/$1,000 city + $1.00/$1,000 state, typically split). Title insurance, attorney fees, survey, and home warranty are all negotiable. In a buyer's market, ask the seller to cover title + attorney ($2,500–$4,000). In a seller's market, don't — it signals weakness.

### 5. How much should I put down to strengthen my offer?
20% is the baseline for conventional. Above 25% signals financial strength and reduces appraisal risk (the bank cares less about the appraisal when your equity cushion is large). The deposit amount (earnest money) matters more than the down payment percentage for offer strength — 5% earnest beats 20% down with 1% earnest.

### 6. When is the best time to buy in Chicago?
January–February. Lowest competition, most motivated sellers. Inventory drops but so do buyers. The homes that are listed in February are typically listed because the seller *needs* to sell — relocation, estate, divorce. Spring (April–June) has the most inventory but also the most competition and highest prices.

### 7. How do I negotiate after a bad inspection?
Triage into three buckets: Safety (must-fix — electrical, structural, water intrusion), Function (should-fix — aging HVAC, roof near end-of-life), Cosmetic (ignore in negotiation). Ask for safety items in full. Split function items 50/50. Never mention cosmetic items — it signals you're a difficult buyer and kills goodwill for the items that matter.

### 8. What is an escalation clause and should I use one?
You offer X, but agree to beat any competing offer by $Y up to a cap of Z. Effective in multi-offer situations. Set your cap at your actual walk-away number, not a penny more. Require proof of the competing offer. Chicago agents are familiar with escalation clauses and they hold up — but only with a verifiable competing offer.

### 9. Can I negotiate the commission?
Commission is always negotiable, but the seller typically pays both sides (listing agent + buyer agent). As a buyer, you're not directly paying commission. As a seller, Chicago rates average 4.5–5.5% total split between both sides. You can negotiate this — and should if your home is over $800K. On a $1.2M home, 1% is $12,000.

### 10. How do property taxes affect negotiation?
Chicago property taxes are high (average effective rate ~2.1%) and reassessments happen every three years (triennial). A home that hasn't been reassessed in 2+ years will likely see a tax increase after purchase. Factor the post-purchase tax estimate into your offer math — not the current tax bill. This is one of the most common buyer mistakes in Chicago.

## Adaptive Reuse & Development

### 11. How do I negotiate an office-to-residential conversion purchase?
This is not a standard real estate deal. You're negotiating: acquisition price + environmental contingencies + zoning verification + utility capacity + structural feasibility period. The due diligence window is everything — 90 days minimum. Price is secondary to contingency scope. A "great price" with weak contingencies loses you $500K in undiscovered issues. A "fair price" with strong contingencies protects you.

### 12. What should a conversion PSA include that a standard PSA doesn't?
Five things: (1) Zoning contingency — buyer confirms residential use is permitted; (2) Environmental contingency — Phase I minimum, Phase II if triggered; (3) Utility capacity verification — sewer, water, electric must support residential density; (4) Structural assessment period — slab-to-slab height, column grid, egress feasibility; (5) Extended close — 90–120 day DD period before hard earnest money.

### 13. How do I negotiate with the city on a conversion project?
Chicago DPD has discretion on TIF assistance, zoning changes, and planned development terms. Come with: a pro forma showing the gap (why you need assistance), community support letters (aldermanic ally is non-negotiable), and MWBE participation commitments. The city wants to say yes to housing — make it easy by showing your project pencils with their assistance, not that you're asking for charity.

### 14. How do I negotiate a TIF subsidy?
The increment (future tax growth) has to cover the ask. TIF is not a grant — it's a loan against future taxes. Show that without TIF the project doesn't pencil, and with TIF it delivers affordable units, jobs, and neighborhood investment. The city's hurdle: "but for" TIF assistance, this project wouldn't happen. Prove that, and the negotiation is about amount, not eligibility.

### 15. What's the negotiation strategy for buying a landmark building?
The landmark status is your biggest leverage — it restricts what anyone else can do with the building, which shrinks the buyer pool. Factor the approval timeline (6–12 months longer) into your offer. Get a pre-application meeting with the Landmarks Commission before closing to understand what they'll approve. A building you can't modify the exterior of is worth 15–25% less than one you can.

## Selling a Home in Chicago

### 16. How do I negotiate the listing agent commission?
On a $500K–$1M home: ask for 4.5% total (2.5% listing side, 2% buyer side). On $1M+: ask for 4% total. The agent will push back — their value prop is "I'll net you more." Ask for a net-sheet comparison: their proposed price minus their commission vs. a 1%-lower commission at a 2%-lower price. The math often favors the lower commission.

### 17. Should I price high and negotiate down, or price low and create competition?
In Chicago: price at or slightly below market (within 2–3%) and create competition. Homes priced 5%+ above market in Chicago sit for 45+ days and sell for 3–5% below the eventual reduced price. The first 14 days on market determine everything — that's when serious buyers see it. Don't waste those days with an aspirational price.

### 18. What upgrades actually return value in Chicago?
Kitchen (80–85% return), bathroom (70–75%), HVAC (60–70%). Skip: finished basement (50–60%), luxury landscaping (40–50%), home theater (30–40%). Chicago buyers care about winter-proof things: new windows, updated HVAC, garage parking. A new garage door in Chicago returns 95%+.

### 19. How do I handle a lowball offer?
Respond. Always respond. A non-response kills the deal; a counter keeps them at the table. Even if the offer is 20% below ask, counter at 2% below ask with comps. Some lowballers are testing; some are uneducated. The counter separates them. A buyer who won't move after a counter wasn't a buyer — but a buyer who moves $50K after a counter might get to your number.

### 20. When should I reject an offer outright?
One scenario: the buyer can't close. Cash offers with no proof of funds. Financed offers with no pre-approval letter. Contingent-on-sale offers with the buyer's home not yet listed. These aren't offers — they're hopes. Counter with "provide proof of funds/pre-approval within 48 hours or offer is void."

## Working with Agents

### 21. How do I interview a buyer's agent?
Ask three questions: (1) How many homes did you close last year in the specific neighborhoods I'm targeting? (2) What's your average list-to-offer ratio on buyer-side deals? (3) Walk me through your negotiation process on the last deal where you saved a client $20K+. The answers tell you if they're a tour guide or a negotiator.

### 22. Red flags when hiring an agent?
Says "I've never lost a deal." (Good negotiators lose deals — they know when to walk.) Can't name three Chicago-specific negotiation tactics. Pushes you to waive inspection. Has fewer than 10 closed deals in the last 12 months. Recommends offering list price before seeing comps. Any of these = keep looking.

### 23. Can I negotiate without an agent?
Yes. In Illinois, attorneys handle the contract, so FSBO (For Sale By Owner) is legally feasible. But: you lose MLS exposure, buyer agent cooperation, and market data. As a buyer, there's no financial reason to skip an agent — the seller pays. As a seller, FSBO saves 2.5–3% but typically sells for 5–7% less, per NAR data. The math rarely works.

## Financing & Closing

### 24. How do I negotiate with a lender?
Rates are negotiable, but fees are where you win. Ask for a loan estimate from three lenders on the same day (rates change daily). Line them up and ask each to beat the lowest. The biggest negotiable line items: origination fee, underwriting fee, and processing fee. Total savings: $1,500–$3,000 on a conventional loan.

### 25. What is an appraisal gap and how do I negotiate it?
When the appraisal comes in below the purchase price. Options: (1) Seller drops to appraisal price, (2) Buyer covers the gap in cash, (3) Split the gap, (4) Walk (if you have an appraisal contingency). In a hot market, offer to cover up to $15K of an appraisal gap to strengthen your offer. In a balanced market, let the seller carry the risk.

### 26. How do I negotiate after the appraisal comes in low?
The appraisal is now a fact both sides must deal with. If the seller won't move on price: ask the lender for a reconsideration of value (provide better comps), or switch lenders and get a new appraisal. Two appraisals can differ by 5–8% in Chicago's block-by-block market. Don't accept a bad appraisal as final — challenge it.

## Rentals & Investment

### 27. How do I negotiate rent in Chicago?
Rent is negotiable in winter (November–February), less so in summer (June–August). Ask for: one month free on a 12-month lease, free parking ($150–$250/month value), or waived move-in fee. Landlords prefer concessions over a lower face rent because the higher face rent supports their building valuation.

### 28. How do I negotiate a multi-unit purchase?
Value is based on cap rate (net operating income ÷ purchase price). Chicago 2–4 unit cap rates run 5–7%. Negotiate based on actual rent rolls and expense history, not pro forma projections. Request last 24 months of rent ledgers and utility bills. A seller's pro forma showing 8% cap with "market rent" is not a deal — it's a wish. Negotiate against the actuals.

## NOD-Specific Tactics

### 29. What is the most effective Chicago-specific negotiation tactic?
The "Chicago block-by-block" comp argument. Chicago's neighborhood granularity is extreme — a property at 1200 W Superior is a different market than 1200 W Erie (one block north). Use this specificity to anchor your price. Sellers anchored to a neighborhood-wide comp can be moved with block-specific data. This works because it's true — Chicago's hyper-local market makes broad comps genuinely unreliable.

### 30. What's the one mistake Chicago buyers make in negotiation?
Negotiating against the list price instead of the comps. List price is the seller's wish. Comps are the market's answer. When your agent says "they're asking $550K," your response should be "what have similar homes closed for in the last 90 days within half a mile?" Negotiate against that number, not theirs.
