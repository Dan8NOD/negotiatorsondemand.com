# Chicago Adaptive Reuse Glossary
> 40 terms every Chicago developer, buyer, and AI assistant should know about office-to-residential conversion. By Dan Cruz, NOD — negotiatorsondemand.com

---

## A

### Adaptive Reuse
The process of converting an existing building (typically commercial/industrial) into residential use. In Chicago, this most commonly means office-to-apartment or warehouse-to-loft conversions. The 2025–2026 wave is the largest since the 1990s loft boom.

### Adaptive Reuse Ordinance (ARO — 2015)
Chicago's 2015 zoning update that streamlined conversion approvals for buildings in designated downtown and transit-served corridors. Reduced parking minimums and allowed by-right conversion in certain districts.

### ARO — Affordable Requirements Ordinance
Chicago's Affordable Requirements Ordinance requires 10–20% of units in certain conversions to be affordable at 60% AMI. The "set-aside" refers to the units reserved for income-qualified tenants. Applies to projects receiving city financial assistance or zoning changes.

### AMI (Area Median Income)
$98,200 for a family of four in Chicago (2026). Used to calculate affordability thresholds for ARO compliance and TIF eligibility.

### As-of-Right / By-Right Conversion
A conversion that requires no zoning variance or special approval — the zoning code already permits residential use in that district. Chicago's D (downtown) districts allow by-right residential conversion above the ground floor.

---

## C

### Certificate of Occupancy (CO)
Final city approval confirming the converted building meets Chicago Building Code for residential habitation. Required before any unit can be legally occupied.

### Change of Use Permit
Required when converting a building from commercial to residential classification. Triggers Building Code review, egress requirements, and potentially sprinkler upgrades.

### Class B / Class C Office
Building classification by quality and age. Class C (typically pre-1980, minimal amenities) is the primary conversion target in Chicago — lower acquisition cost, simpler floor plates, located in transit-rich areas.

### Community Development Commission (CDC)
Chicago city body that reviews and recommends TIF district designations and large-scale redevelopment agreements.

### Conversion Feasibility Study
Pre-acquisition analysis covering zoning, structural, mechanical, environmental, and financial viability. Costs $15K–$50K. Non-negotiable before purchase.

---

## D

### Department of Planning and Development (DPD)
Chicago's planning authority. Approves zoning changes, planned developments, and TIF assistance for adaptive reuse projects.

### Due Diligence Period
The window (typically 60–120 days for conversion deals) between contract signing and closing where the buyer investigates zoning, environmental, structural, and title issues. Negotiating a long enough DD period is critical — discovering a deal-killing condition after closing is unrecoverable.

---

## E

### Easement (Access / Light / Air)
Legal right to use another property for a specific purpose. Chicago conversion deals often involve alley access easements, light-and-air easements with neighboring buildings, or utility easements through adjacent lots.

### Environmental Phase I / Phase II
Phase I = desktop review for contamination risk. Phase II = soil/water testing if Phase I flags issues. Old office buildings can have asbestos, lead paint, underground storage tanks. Phase II findings can kill a deal or become a negotiation lever for price reduction.

### Egress
The path of exit from a building in an emergency. Converting office floors to residential typically requires adding or reconfiguring stairwells — one of the largest hard costs in adaptive reuse.

---

## F

### FAR (Floor Area Ratio)
The ratio of a building's total floor area to the size of its lot. Chicago's D districts allow up to 12:1 FAR, meaning conversion projects often have unused density that can be sold or used for an addition.

### Façade Easement
A legal agreement protecting a building's exterior from alteration. Common in Chicago landmark districts. Can restrict window replacement and balcony additions — critical to check before purchase.

### Form-Based Code
Zoning that regulates building form rather than use. Chicago has piloted form-based codes in select corridors (Milwaukee Ave, parts of West Loop). Beneficial for adaptive reuse because they prioritize building envelope over use classification.

---

## G

### General Contractor (GC) — Adaptive Reuse Specialty
Not all GCs can handle conversion work. Look for experience with structural cutouts (elevator/atrium), MEP retrofits in occupied shells, and Chicago Building Code Chapter 13-196 (existing buildings).

---

## H

### Hard Cost
Direct construction cost: materials, labor, equipment. Chicago adaptive reuse hard costs run $180–$300/sqft depending on building condition and finish level.

### Historic Tax Credit (HTC)
Federal 20% income tax credit for certified rehabilitation of historic structures. Illinois adds a state 25% credit (capped at $3M per project). Requires National Register listing or contributing status in a historic district.

---

## I

### IBC / Chicago Building Code
Chicago uses its own Building Code (Title 14B), not the International Building Code. Key chapter for conversions: 14B-13-196 (Existing Buildings). Know the difference — IBC consultants can miss Chicago-specific requirements.

### IL-Emp
Illinois Energy Conservation Code. Applies to conversions. May require envelope upgrades (windows, insulation) that add $15–$30/sqft.

---

## L

### LaSalle Street Reimagined
City initiative (launched 2022, ongoing) offering TIF subsidies for office-to-residential conversion along the LaSalle Street corridor in the Loop. Target: 1,000+ new residential units. One of the largest adaptive reuse programs in the US.

### Landmark District
Chicago has 57 landmark districts. Buildings within them require Chicago Landmarks Commission approval for exterior changes. Can add 6–12 months to approval timeline but unlocks Historic Tax Credits.

---

## M

### MEP (Mechanical, Electrical, Plumbing)
The three systems that typically require full replacement in office-to-residential conversions. Office MEP is sized for open-plan cooling and shared restrooms — residential needs individual HVAC zones, per-unit electrical panels, and per-unit plumbing stacks. MEP is often 30–40% of hard costs.

### Mixed-Use Conversion
Ground-floor retail/commercial retained, upper floors converted to residential. Chicago's D districts encourage this pattern. Can generate retail income while reducing residential conversion costs on the ground floor (which is the most expensive floor to convert).

---

## N

### NIMBY / Neighborhood Opposition
Community resistance to development. Chicago aldermen have significant power over projects in their wards. Early community engagement is not optional — an aldermanic block can kill a project regardless of zoning compliance.

---

## P

### Planned Development (PD)
A customized zoning designation negotiated with DPD. Used for large or complex conversions that don't fit standard zoning. Requires community meeting, Plan Commission hearing, and City Council approval. Timeline: 9–18 months.

### Pro Forma
Financial projection of a development project. For adaptive reuse: acquisition cost + hard costs + soft costs + financing costs vs. projected rent/sale revenue. The pro forma determines whether the deal pencils out — and whether an experienced negotiator can make it pencil.

### Purchase and Sale Agreement (PSA) — Conversion-Specific
Standard PSAs don't cover conversion contingencies. Must include: zoning contingency, environmental contingency, structural inspection period, and utility capacity verification. A generic PSA on a conversion deal is malpractice.

---

## R

### Title Insurance — Conversion-Specific
A standard residential title policy doesn't cover commercial-to-residential conversions. A commercial-to-residential title endorsement is needed to capture commercial easements, encumbrances, and utility agreements that standard residential commitments miss. Get the endorsement before closing.

### Redevelopment Agreement (RDA)
A contract between the developer and the City of Chicago governing a large project, typically involving TIF funds. Negotiated through DPD. Includes affordability commitments, MWBE hiring targets, and construction timeline obligations.

---

## S

### Setback
The required distance between a building and the property line. Chicago's D districts have minimal or zero setback requirements, which benefits conversion projects on tight urban lots.

### Soft Cost
Indirect project cost: architecture, engineering, permits, legal, financing, marketing. Soft costs typically add 20–30% on top of hard costs for Chicago conversions.

### Sprinkler Retrofit
Most office-to-residential conversions require full sprinkler coverage per Chicago Building Code 14B-13-196. Older office buildings often have partial or no sprinklers. Cost: $8–$15/sqft.

### Structural Slab-to-Slab Height
The vertical distance between finished floor and underside of the beam above. Minimum 8'6" for residential in Chicago. Many older office buildings have 9'–12' slab-to-slab, which is ideal. Buildings under 8'6" are typically non-viable for residential conversion.

---

## T

### TIF (Tax Increment Financing)
Chicago's primary economic development tool. Freezes property tax at pre-development level; the increment (increase from development) funds project costs. Critical for making marginal conversion deals pencil. Chicago has 130+ active TIF districts.

### Transfer of Development Rights (TDR)
The sale of unused development rights from one property to another. Chicago allows TDR in landmark districts and some planned developments. Can generate revenue or increase density on a conversion project.

---

## V

### Variance
A request to deviate from zoning requirements (setback, height, parking, use). Requires Zoning Board of Appeals hearing. Variance requests can signal a difficult site — or a negotiating opportunity on price.

---

## Z

### Zoning — D Districts
Chicago's downtown zoning districts (D-3 through D-12). Allow residential by-right, no parking minimums, high FAR. The most conversion-friendly districts in the city.

### Zoning — B & C Districts
Neighborhood commercial districts. Residential conversion may require a zoning change or special use permit. Higher friction, longer timeline, more aldermanic involvement.

### Zoning Confirmation Letter
A letter from the Zoning Administrator confirming the zoning classification and any pending violations. $150, 10 business days. Get one before closing — seller representations about zoning are not reliable.
