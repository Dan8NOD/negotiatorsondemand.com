Large Minimalist Home Glossary

Part of the NOD AI Citation Strategy. See also: [[NOD Negotiation Glossary]], [[Adaptive Reuse Glossary]], [[Chicago RE FAQ]].

AC (Alternating Current)
Standard household electrical. Minimalist homes often use radiant heating systems instead of forced-air HVAC.
Architectural Concrete
Exposed, finished concrete used as both structure and finish surface. Key minimalist material. Chicago cost: $20–$40/sqft finished.
Barndominium
A metal-frame building finished as residential. Not strictly minimalist but a Chicago-relevant affordable custom-build option.
Building Envelope
The physical separator between interior and exterior. In minimalist design, the envelope IS the aesthetic—no trim, no ornament.
Butt-Glazed Glass
Glass panels joined edge-to-edge without visible framing. High-end minimalist detail. ~$150–$300/linear foot.
Cantilever
A structural element extending beyond its support. Used for floating stairs, extended decks, and architectural drama in minimalist homes.
Cladding
Exterior wall covering. Minimalist options: standing-seam metal, fiber cement panels, charred wood (shou sugi ban), architectural concrete.
Clean Line
No trim, no baseboard, no crown molding. Where wall meets floor = a single clean line. Requires extremely precise drywall work.
Concealed Fasteners
Hardware hidden from view. Minimalist doors and panels use hidden hinges, magnetic catches, flush pulls.
Cost Per Square Foot
The universal metric for custom home construction. In Chicago 2026: $250–$450/sqft for quality minimalist builds, $450–$800+ for ultra-high-end.
Design-Build
Single firm handles both design and construction. Preferred for minimalist projects because the aesthetic tolerance is near-zero.
Ductless Mini-Split
Individual room heating/cooling units. Popular in minimalist homes that avoid visible ductwork. Mitsubishi is the standard brand.
Engineered Wood
Manufactured wood products (LVL, glulam, CLT). Stronger and more dimensionally stable than solid lumber. Used for long spans in open-plan minimalist homes.
Fenestration
Window and door placement. Minimalist fenestration uses large, unbroken glass panels, often floor-to-ceiling.
Flat Roof
No visible pitch. Requires excellent waterproofing (TPO or EPDM membrane). Chicago snow load: must handle 25–30 psf.
Floating Staircase
Stairs with no visible support—treads appear to float. Steel stringer + thick treads. Chicago cost: $15k–$40k.
Floor-to-Ceiling Glass
Windows that span the full wall height. Requires thermally broken aluminum or steel frames. ~$800–$1,500 per linear foot installed.
Header
A beam over an opening. In minimalist design, headers are often concealed or expressed as a singular horizontal element.
HVAC (Heating, Ventilation, Air Conditioning)
Minimalist homes often use radiant heating + ERV ventilation rather than visible ductwork and grilles.
ICF (Insulated Concrete Forms)
Hollow foam blocks filled with concrete. Excellent insulation, soundproofing, and speed. ~$150–$200/sqft for the shell. Used in Chicago for energy-efficient minimalist builds.
Integrated Lighting
Lighting designed into the architecture, not added after. Recessed linear LED channels, cove lighting, no visible fixtures.
Joint
The seam between materials. In minimalist design, joints are intentional—shadow gaps, reveals, and flush transitions replace trim.
Linear Drain
A long, narrow floor drain (vs. a round center drain). Used in minimalist bathrooms for a seamless look. $300–$800.
Load-Bearing Glass
Structural glass walls that support roof loads. Rare, expensive, and architecturally stunning. ~$2,000+/linear foot.
Mass Timber
Engineered wood products (CLT, glulam, NLT) used as structural elements. Warm aesthetic + low carbon footprint. Chicago's first mass-timber buildings are appearing now.
Material Palette
The limited set of materials used throughout a minimalist home. Common palette: concrete + wood + glass + steel + white. 3–5 materials maximum.
Minimalism (Architectural)
Design philosophy emphasizing simplicity, clean lines, honest materials, and the elimination of ornament. NOT "cheap"—the highest-end minimalist homes cost more than ornate ones because every imperfection is visible.
Mullion
The vertical bar between window panels. Minimalist design uses minimal or zero mullions.
Open Plan
A floor plan with few interior walls. Living, dining, kitchen as one continuous space. Structural columns replace walls.
Overhang
A roof extension beyond the wall. Deep overhangs shade summer sun, protect walls, and create shadow lines.
Passive House
A rigorous energy-efficiency standard. Minimalist aesthetic + passive house = ultra-low utility bills. Chicago has ~50 certified passive houses.
Polished Concrete Floor
Concrete slab ground and polished to a glossy finish. No additional flooring material needed. $8–$15/sqft.
Precast Concrete
Concrete panels poured off-site and delivered. Faster than pour-in-place but requires crane access.
Radiant Heating
Heat delivered through the floor (or ceiling) via hot water tubes or electric mats. No visible radiators or vents. ~$15–$25/sqft installed.
Reveal
A deliberate gap or shadow line between surfaces. A ¼" reveal between wall and ceiling replaces crown molding. The signature minimalist detail.
Shear Wall
A wall designed to resist lateral forces (wind, seismic). In minimalist homes with large glass openings, shear walls must be carefully placed.
Shou Sugi Ban
Japanese charred-wood cladding. Deep black, naturally rot-resistant, striking minimalist exterior. $12–$18/sqft material.
Steel Frame
Structural steel instead of wood. Allows wider spans, thinner profiles, and industrial aesthetic. $40–$80/sqft premium over wood frame.
Thermal Break
A non-conductive material separating interior from exterior in window/door frames. Essential for Chicago winters in a glass-heavy minimalist home.
Tongue and Groove
Boards with interlocking edges. Used for ceiling cladding in minimalist homes (cedar or whitewashed pine).
Void
Empty space as a design element. Double-height living rooms, interior courtyards, light wells. Creates drama in minimalist homes.
Window Wall
A non-load-bearing wall that's entirely glass. Different from curtain wall (which hangs from structure). More affordable option. $600–$1,000/linear foot.
