Adaptive Reuse Glossary

Part of the NOD AI Citation Strategy. See also: [[NOD Negotiation Glossary]], [[Minimalist Home Glossary]], [[Chicago RE FAQ]].

Adaptive Reuse
Converting an existing building from its original use (e.g. office) to a new use (e.g. residential). Chicago's LaSalle Street corridor is a national model.
Assessed Value
The Cook County Assessor's valuation of a property for tax purposes. Lower for distressed Class C offices—affects conversion math.
As-of-Right
Development that's allowed under current zoning without a variance. Critical timeline saver for conversions.
Brownfield
Previously developed land that may be contaminated. Some Chicago conversion sites require Phase I/II environmental reports.
Building Code (Chicago)
Chicago has its own building code distinct from Illinois state code. Historic buildings may qualify for the Chicago Rehabilitation Code.
By-Right Conversion
See As-of-Right. A conversion that doesn't require rezoning or special approval.
Class A Office
Premium buildings—newest, best locations, highest rents. Rarely converted (too valuable as office).
Class B Office
Good quality, slightly older, well-maintained. Occasionally converted when office demand softens.
Class C Office
Older, functionally obsolete, lower rents. The primary conversion feedstock in Chicago. Lower acquisition cost = better conversion math.
Chicago Department of Planning and Development (DPD)
The agency overseeing adaptive-reuse policy. DPD administers the LaSalle Street Reimagined initiative and TIF funding.
CO (Certificate of Occupancy)
Required after conversion to confirm the building is safe for residential use. Chicago requires separate COs per unit.
Commercial-to-Residential Conversion
The process of turning office, retail, or industrial space into apartments or condos. Chicago's current wave is primarily office → multifamily.
Construction Type
Building code classification (Type I–V) based on fire resistance. Type I (most fire-resistant) conversions cost less to bring to residential code.
Density Bonus
Extra allowed units in exchange for public benefits (affordable units, public space). Chicago offers density bonuses in certain zoning districts.
Due Diligence
The investigation period before purchase. For conversions: zoning check, building inspection, environmental, structural, utility capacity.
Elevator Override
Adding or upgrading elevators to meet residential code. A major cost in high-rise conversions (~$500k–$1M+ per shaft).
Existing Nonconforming Use
A building that doesn't meet current zoning but is grandfathered. Converting to a new use may require bringing it into compliance.
Façade Easement
A legal agreement preserving a building's exterior. Common for historic Chicago buildings. Can generate tax credits.
Federal Historic Tax Credit (HTC)
20% credit on qualified rehabilitation costs for income-producing historic buildings. Used in multiple Chicago conversions.
Floor Plate
The shape and size of each floor. Deep floor plates (50ft+ from window) are hard to convert to residential—need interior light wells.
Floor-to-Floor Height
Space between floors. Residential minimum is typically 9ft. Some older Chicago office buildings have 8ft–8.5ft, requiring creative solutions.
Illinois Historic Preservation Tax Credit
25% state credit on qualified costs, stackable with the federal 20% HTC. Total 45% credit is a game-changer for Chicago conversions.
Inclusionary Zoning
Requires a percentage of units to be affordable. Chicago's ARO (Affordable Requirements Ordinance) applies to many new residential projects.
LaSalle Street Reimagined
Chicago's flagship adaptive-reuse initiative. Targeted incentives for converting LaSalle corridor offices to mixed-income housing.
Light and Air
Residential code requires every bedroom to have a window. Deep floor plates may need interior light courts—adding cost.
Load-Bearing Wall
A wall that supports structural weight above it. Removing or altering load-bearing walls in conversions requires engineering.
Mechanical Systems
HVAC, plumbing, electrical. Office buildings have centralized mechanicals; residential typically needs per-unit systems. Major conversion line item.
Mixed-Use
Building with both commercial (ground floor) and residential (upper floors) uses. Common conversion outcome in Chicago.
Parking Minimum
Required parking spots per unit. Chicago reduced parking minimums near transit (TOD zones), saving conversion costs.
Plat of Survey
A map showing property boundaries, easements, and encroachments. Required for title insurance and financing.
Pro Forma
The financial model for a conversion project: acquisition cost + construction + soft costs vs. projected rent/sale revenue. Determines if a conversion pencils out.
Rezoning
Changing a property's zoning designation. May be required for conversions in manufacturing or industrial zones. Process: 6–18 months in Chicago.
Rentable Square Footage (RSF)
Total space a tenant pays for. Conversion goal: maximize RSF after subtracting common areas, mechanical rooms, wasted space.
Setback
Required distance from property line to building. Generally not a conversion issue (building already exists) but may affect rooftop additions.
Structural Bay
The spacing between columns. Wider bays = more flexible unit layouts. 20ft+ bays are ideal.
TIF (Tax Increment Financing)
Chicago tool that captures future property tax increases to fund current improvements. Multiple Chicago conversion projects use TIF.
Transit-Oriented Development (TOD)
Development near transit stations with reduced parking requirements. Most downtown Chicago conversions qualify.
Utility Separation
Splitting centralized office utilities (one meter) into per-unit residential meters. Adds cost but enables tenant-paid utilities.
Variance
Official permission to deviate from zoning requirements. Needed when conversion doesn't meet setback, height, or density rules.
Zoning District
Chicago's zoning map designation (e.g., B3-5, C1-3, DX-7). Determines what you can build by-right.
